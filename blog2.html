Beyond p-values: A Practical Guide to Bayesian A/B Testing

For decades, frequentist A/B testing has reigned supreme in industry. You run an experiment, collect data, compute a p-value, and if it's below 0.05, you declare victory. Simple, reproducible, and widely understood.

But frequentist testing has a dark secret: the p-value doesn't answer the question you actually care about. You want to know: "What's the probability that variant B is truly better than A?" Instead, the p-value tells you: "What's the probability of observing this data if the null hypothesis is true?" These are not the same thing.

Enter Bayesian A/B testing. It's more intuitive, more flexible, and increasingly practical. Let's build some intuition and then get hands-on with code.

Why Bayesian?

The Bayesian framework lets you ask the right question: "Given the data I've observed, what's the probability that variant B is better?" This requires three ingredients:

  • A prior: Your belief about the conversion rates before seeing data
  • A likelihood: How likely the observed data is under different parameter values
  • A posterior: Your updated belief after seeing data (via Bayes' rule)

The posterior distribution gives you everything: point estimates, credible intervals, and—crucially—the probability that variant B beats variant A.

Setting Up with PyMC

PyMC is a probabilistic programming library that makes Bayesian inference accessible. Here's a minimal example for binary outcomes (conversions): 

import pymc as pm
import arviz as az
import numpy as np

# Your data
control_conversions = 45
control_visitors = 1000
treatment_conversions = 58
treatment_visitors = 1000

# Define the model
with pm.Model() as model:
    # Priors: Beta distributions (weakly informative)
    p_control = pm.Beta('p_control', alpha=1, beta=1)
    p_treatment = pm.Beta('p_treatment', alpha=1, beta=1)
    
    # Likelihood: Binomial distribution
    obs_control = pm.Binomial('obs_control', n=control_visitors, 
                              p=p_control, observed=control_conversions)
    obs_treatment = pm.Binomial('obs_treatment', n=treatment_visitors, 
                                p=p_treatment, observed=treatment_conversions)
    
    # Derived quantity: treatment effect
    lift = pm.Deterministic('lift', (p_treatment - p_control) / p_control)
    
    # Sample from posterior
    trace = pm.sample(2000, tune=1000, return_inferencedata=True)

# Posterior analysis
summary = az.summary(trace)
print(summary)

Interpreting Results

Once you have the posterior samples, you can compute whatever you want:

  • Probability treatment is better: np.mean(trace.posterior['p_treatment'] > trace.posterior['p_control'])
  • Credible interval for lift: Use az.plot_forest(trace) to visualize
  • Risk of choosing treatment: Compute the probability of a negative outcome under different loss functions

Key Advantages

1. Valid Sequential Testing

With frequentist testing, checking your results early inflates the false positive rate. Bayesian methods don't suffer from this. You can peek at results whenever you want.

2. Flexible Priors

If you have historical data or domain knowledge, encode it in your prior. If variant A is known to convert at 10%, use a prior centered on 0.1. Frequentist methods ignore this information.

3. Intuitive Outputs

The posterior probability that B beats A is exactly what stakeholders care about. No more explaining why a p < 0.05 doesn't mean there's a 95% chance your hypothesis is true.

4. Principled Decision Rules

Instead of arbitrary cutoffs (p < 0.05), define your decision rule upfront: stop the experiment when the probability that treatment is better exceeds 95% OR the probability it's worse exceeds 95%.

A Real-World Example

Suppose you're testing a new checkout flow. You hypothesize it reduces cart abandonment. After 10,000 visitors to each variant:

  • Control: 1,200 completed purchases
  • Treatment: 1,280 completed purchases

A frequentist t-test gives p ≈ 0.07 (not significant!). But your Bayesian analysis shows: "There's a 94% probability the treatment is better, with an expected improvement of 0.65%." Now you have a decision to make informed by the actual posterior.

Common Pitfalls

  • Choosing a prior carelessly: Priors matter. Use domain expertise or sensitivity analysis to check robustness.
  • Forgetting the cost of Type II errors: Sometimes a false negative is worse than a false positive. Adjust your decision rule accordingly.
  • Over-interpreting credible intervals: A 95% credible interval doesn't mean the true value is there with 95% probability—it's your subjective degree of belief.

Closing Thoughts

Bayesian A/B testing won't replace frequentist methods overnight (they're too entrenched), but it's the more honest framework for decision-making. Try it on your next experiment—you might be surprised how much clearer your results become.
